# Comprehensive US English Natural Language Grammar

This grammar provides extensive coverage of American English language structures at the same level as the Danish grammar, with over 1,300 lines of comprehensive grammar rules.

## Grammar Size & Scope

- **1,300+ lines** of comprehensive grammar rules
- **Comparable to Danish** (1,393 lines) in depth and breadth
- **Production-ready** for advanced natural language processing applications
- **Supports nested sub-sentences** up to 3+ levels of embedding
- **Five regional dialects**: General American, Southern, New England, Midwestern, AAVE

## Core Features

### 1. Complete Verb System

#### Regular Verb Conjugations
- **Present tense**: base form, third person singular -s (work, works)
- **Past tense**: base + -ed (worked, studied)
- **Present perfect**: have/has + past participle (have worked, has studied)
- **Past perfect**: had + past participle (had worked)
- **Future tense**: will/shall + base form (will work)
- **Conditional mood**: would + base form (would work)

#### Irregular Verb Conjugations (100+ verbs)
Including: be, have, do, go, say, get, make, know, think, take, see, come, want, look, use, find, give, tell, work, call, try, ask, need, feel, become, leave, put, mean, keep, let, begin, seem, help, talk, turn, start, show, hear, play, run, move, live, believe, hold, bring, happen, write, provide, sit, stand, lose, pay, meet, include, continue, set, learn, change, lead, understand, watch, follow, stop, create, speak, read, spend, grow, open, walk, win, offer, remember, love, consider, appear, buy, wait, serve, die, send, build, stay, fall, cut, reach, kill, remain, suggest, raise, pass, sell, decide, return, explain, hope, develop, carry, break, receive, agree, support, hit, produce, eat, cover, catch, draw

#### Voice and Aspect
- **Active voice**: Standard form
- **Passive voice**: be + past participle (is written, was built)
- **Progressive aspect**: be + present participle (am working, was studying)
- **Perfect aspect**: have + past participle (have finished, had left)
- **Perfect progressive**: have been + present participle (have been waiting)

### 2. Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) Word Order

American English follows strict SVO word order:
- **Subject** (noun phrase or pronoun)
- **Verb** (main verb with optional auxiliaries)
- **Object** (direct and/or indirect)
- **Adverbials** (manner, place, time)

Examples:
```
I read books every day.
She has been studying English for two years.
They will send you the information tomorrow.
```

### 3. Comprehensive Noun Phrase Structure

#### Articles
- **Indefinite articles**: a, an (a dog, an apple)
- **Definite article**: the (the dog, the apple)

#### Number
- **Singular**: Regular forms (dog, cat, house)
- **Plural**: Multiple patterns (-s, -es, -ies, -ves, irregular)
  - Regular: dogs, cats, houses
  - -es: boxes, churches, dishes
  - -ies: cities, babies, parties
  - -ves: knives, lives, wolves
  - Irregular: men, women, children, feet, teeth

#### Compound Nouns
Productive compounding system:
- **Noun + Noun**: bookstore, football, newspaper
- **Adjective + Noun**: blackboard, software, greenhouse
- **Hyphenated**: mother-in-law, editor-in-chief

#### Genitive Case
- **Singular possession**: John's book, the cat's tail
- **Plural possession**: the students' books, the cats' tails

### 4. Comprehensive Pronoun System

#### Personal Pronouns (with case)
- **Nominative**: I, you, he, she, it, we, they
- **Accusative**: me, you, him, her, it, us, them

#### Reflexive Pronouns
- myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves

#### Reciprocal Pronouns
- each other, one another

#### Possessive Pronouns
- **Determiners**: my, your, his, her, its, our, their
- **Independent**: mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs

#### Demonstrative Pronouns
- **Singular**: this, that
- **Plural**: these, those

#### Indefinite Pronouns
- someone, somebody, something, anyone, anybody, anything, everyone, everybody, everything, no one, nobody, nothing, one, some, any, all, each, every, both, few, many, several, none, another, other, others

### 5. Adjective System with Comparison

#### Agreement
English adjectives don't change form for number or gender:
- **Single form**: big, small, beautiful, interesting

#### Comparison
- **Positive**: big, good, interesting
- **Comparative**: 
  - Short adjectives: bigger, smaller (-er suffix)
  - Long adjectives: more interesting, more beautiful
  - Irregular: better, worse, more, less, further/farther
- **Superlative**:
  - Short adjectives: the biggest, the smallest (-est suffix)
  - Long adjectives: the most interesting, the most beautiful
  - Irregular: the best, the worst, the most, the least

### 6. Complex Sentence Structures

#### Subordinate Clauses
- **That-clauses**: I know that she is coming
- **Relative clauses**: The man who lives here, the book that I read
- **Interrogative clauses**: She asked where he was going

#### Adverbial Clauses (7 types)
1. **Temporal**: when, while, as, after, before, since, until
2. **Causal**: because, since, as, now that
3. **Conditional**: if, unless, provided that, in case
4. **Concessive**: although, though, even though, even if
5. **Purpose**: so that, in order that, in order to
6. **Result**: so that, such that
7. **Comparative**: as, than, as if, as though

### 7. Modal Verbs

Full set of modal auxiliaries:
- **Ability**: can, could
- **Permission**: may, might, can, could
- **Obligation**: must, should, ought to
- **Possibility**: may, might, could
- **Willingness**: will, would
- **Necessity**: must, have to, need to

### 8. Question Formation

#### Yes/No Questions
- Auxiliary inversion: Are you coming? Did she leave? Will they arrive?

#### Wh-Questions
- Question word + auxiliary + subject + verb: Where are you going? What did he say?

#### Tag Questions
- Declarative + opposite tag: You're coming, aren't you? She won't be late, will she?

### 9. Negation Patterns

- **Standard negation**: not, n't (do not, don't, cannot, can't)
- **Never**: He has never been there
- **No/None**: I have no money, None of them came
- **Nothing/Nobody**: I saw nothing, Nobody knows

### 11. Nested Sub-Sentences and Complex Structures

#### Nested Subordinate Clauses
Support for multiple levels of clause embedding:
- **2-level nesting**: "I think that he said that she is coming"
- **3-level nesting**: "He knows that I think that she said that they are coming"
- **4+ level nesting**: Supported with readability warnings

#### Coordinated Subordinate Clauses
- "He said that he would come and that he would help us"
- "I don't know whether he is coming or whether she is going"

#### Nested Relative Clauses
- "The man who I met told me that his son will come home"
- "The book which was written by the author who won the prize is excellent"

#### Multi-Embedded Sentences
- Main clause + multiple subordinate clauses at different levels
- Complex patterns with mixed clause types

#### Parenthetical Clauses
- "He is, I think, the best candidate"
- "The book, which was expensive, is worth it"

### 14. Regional Dialectal Variations (5 Dialects)

The grammar includes support for five major US English regional varieties.

#### 1. General American (Standard US English)
Standard variety used in media and education across most of the US.

#### 2. Southern US English
Distinctive features of the American South:
- **Pronouns**: y'all (you all), all y'all, you'uns
- **Examples**: "Y'all are coming, right?" "All y'all need to listen"

#### 3. New England English
Features of northeastern states:
- **Pronouns**: yous, you guys
- **Examples**: "Where are yous going?" "You guys want coffee?"

#### 4. Midwestern English
Features of the Midwest/Great Lakes region:
- **Pronouns**: you guys, yous guys
- **Examples**: "You guys should come over" "Yous guys are funny"

#### 5. African American Vernacular English (AAVE)
Important variety with systematic grammatical patterns:
- **Aspectual markers**: done, been, be
- **Habitual be**: "She be working" (she habitually works)
- **Completive done**: "They done finished" (they have finished)
- **Remote past been**: "She been working" (she has been working for a long time)

### 15. Context-Sensitive Validation (11 rules)

1. **Subject-verb agreement**: Subject and verb must agree in number and person
2. **Pronoun case**: Nominative for subjects, accusative for objects
3. **Article-noun compatibility**: "a" vs "an" based on sound
4. **Reflexive pronoun agreement**: Must match subject in person and number
5. **Tense consistency**: Consistent tense usage within sentences
6. **Double negative**: Warning about non-standard multiple negatives
7. **Passive voice formation**: Proper use of be + past participle
8. **Modal verb + infinitive**: Modals followed by base form
9. **Comparative context**: Comparative adjectives with "than"
10. **Nested clause depth**: Warnings for deep nesting (>3 levels)

## Technical Implementation

- **Format**: CEBNF (Context-Enhanced Backus-Naur Form)
- **Token splitting**: Space-based for natural language
- **Context attributes**: 
  - @CASE[nominative|accusative]
  - @NUMBER[singular|plural]
  - @PERSON[first|second|third]
  - @TENSE[present|past|future|present-perfect|past-perfect|future-perfect]
  - @ASPECT[progressive|perfect]
  - @MOOD[modal|conditional]
  - @VOICE[active|passive]
  - @DEGREE[positive|comparative|superlative]
  - @IRREGULAR (marker for irregular forms)
  - @REFLEXIVE (marker for reflexive pronouns)
  - @DIALECT[general_american|southern|new_england|midwestern|aave]

- **Symbol table**: Hierarchical sharing for cross-reference validation
- **Error levels**: ERROR (hard violations), WARNING (stylistic issues)
