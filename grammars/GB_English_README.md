# Comprehensive GB English (British English) Natural Language Grammar

This grammar provides extensive coverage of British English language structures at the same level as the Danish grammar, with over 1,400 lines of comprehensive grammar rules and British-specific features.

## Grammar Size & Scope

- **1,400+ lines** of comprehensive grammar rules
- **Comparable to Danish** (1,393 lines) in depth and breadth
- **Production-ready** for advanced natural language processing applications
- **Supports nested sub-sentences** up to 3+ levels of embedding
- **Six regional dialects**: Received Pronunciation, Cockney, Geordie, Scouse, Brummie, Scottish

## Core Features

### 1. Complete Verb System

#### Regular Verb Conjugations
- **Present tense**: base form, third person singular -s (work, works)
- **Past tense**: base + -ed (worked, studied)
- **Present perfect**: have/has + past participle (have worked, has studied)
- **Past perfect**: had + past participle (had worked)
- **Future tense**: will/shall + base form (will work, shall work)
- **Conditional mood**: would/should + base form (would work, should work)

#### British-Specific Past Tense Forms
Unlike American English, British English commonly uses -t endings:
- **learnt** (vs. learned), **spelt** (vs. spelled), **burnt** (vs. burned)
- **dreamt** (vs. dreamed), **leant** (vs. leaned), **leapt** (vs. leaped)
- **spilt** (vs. spilled), **spoilt** (vs. spoiled), **dwelt** (vs. dwelled)
- **smelt** (vs. smelled)

#### Irregular Verb Conjugations (100+ verbs)
Including all standard irregular verbs plus British variants.

#### Voice and Aspect
- **Active voice**: Standard form
- **Passive voice**: be + past participle (is written, was built)
- **Progressive aspect**: be + present participle (am working, was studying)
- **Perfect aspect**: have + past participle (have finished, had left)
- **Perfect progressive**: have been + present participle (have been waiting)

### 2. British Spelling Patterns

The grammar includes comprehensive British spelling support:

#### -our vs -or
- colour, favour, honour, labour, neighbour, rumour, humour, behaviour, flavour

#### -re vs -er
- centre, theatre, metre, litre, fibre, calibre, sombre, lustre

#### -ce vs -se
- defence, offence, licence (noun), pretence

#### -ogue vs -og
- programme, catalogue, dialogue, analogue, monologue, epilogue, travelogue

#### -ise vs -ize
British English traditionally uses -ise (though -ize is also acceptable):
- apologise, organise, realise, recognise, analyse, paralyse, emphasise, summarise, prioritise, finalise, utilise, mobilise, minimise, maximise, criticise, characterise, categorise, centralise, modernise, privatise, nationalise, civilise, memorise, authorise, capitalise, equalise, generalise, legalise, localise, naturalise, normalise, personalise, popularise, rationalise, sensitise, socialise, stabilise, symbolise, sympathise, synchronise, theorise, visualise

#### Other British Spellings
- **grey** (vs. gray)
- **cheque** (vs. check - bank)
- **lorry** (vs. truck)
- **boot** (vs. trunk - car)
- **bonnet** (vs. hood - car)
- **windscreen** (vs. windshield)
- **petrol** (vs. gasoline/gas)
- **pavement** (vs. sidewalk)
- **lift** (vs. elevator)
- **flat** (vs. apartment)
- **torch** (vs. flashlight)
- **biscuit** (vs. cookie)
- **holiday** (vs. vacation)
- **autumn** (vs. fall)
- **queue/queueing** (vs. line/lining up)

### 3. British Present Participles

British English often doubles consonants before -ing:
- travelling, cancelling, signalling, modelling, labelling, levelling, quarrelling, marvelling, panelling, channelling, counselling, equalling, rivalling, totalling, spiralling

### 4. Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) Word Order

British English follows strict SVO word order (same as US English):
- **Subject** (noun phrase or pronoun)
- **Verb** (main verb with optional auxiliaries)
- **Object** (direct and/or indirect)
- **Adverbials** (manner, place, time)

### 5. Comprehensive Noun Phrase Structure

Same structure as US English, with British spelling variants integrated.

### 6. Comprehensive Pronoun System

#### Personal Pronouns (with case)
- **Nominative**: I, you, he, she, it, we, they
- **Accusative**: me, you, him, her, it, us, them

#### Reflexive Pronouns
- myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves

#### Reciprocal Pronouns
- each other, one another

#### Possessive Pronouns
- **Determiners**: my, your, his, her, its, our, their
- **Independent**: mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs

### 7. Adjective System with Comparison

British English follows the same comparison patterns as US English, with British spelling integrated.

### 8. Complex Sentence Structures

Full support for all complex sentence types, with British-specific conjunctions:
- **whilst** (instead of while) - "Whilst I agree, I think we should wait"
- **amongst** (instead of among) - "He walked amongst the crowd"

### 9. Modal Verbs with British Forms

British English has additional modal contractions:
- **shan't** (shall not) - "I shan't be long"
- **mayn't** (may not) - less common but grammatically valid
- **needn't** (need not) - "You needn't worry"

Examples:
- "I shall go tomorrow" (British formal)
- "We shan't forget this"
- "You needn't bring anything"

### 10. Question Formation

#### Tag Questions
British English uses tag questions more frequently than American English:
- "You're coming, aren't you?"
- "She won't be late, will she?"
- "They haven't finished, have they?"
- "We shall see them, shan't we?"

### 11. Relative Pronouns

British English sometimes uses additional formal relative pronouns:
- **whence** (from where - archaic/formal) - "the place whence he came"
- **whereby** (by which) - "a system whereby we can communicate"

### 12. Prepositions

British English has the additional preposition:
- **whilst** - used more commonly in British English than US

### 13. Adverbs

British English uses some adverbs differently:
- **presently** (soon, in a moment) vs. US (currently)
- **quite** (completely in British, fairly in US)
- **awfully** (very - informal British)
- **jolly** (very - informal British) - "That's jolly good!"

### 14. Regional Dialectal Variations (6 Dialects)

The grammar includes support for six major British English regional varieties.

#### 1. Received Pronunciation (RP)
Standard British English used in education and media.

#### 2. Cockney (London East End)
Distinctive features:
- **Pronouns**: meself, yerself, hisself, theirselves
- **Examples**: "I done it meself", "Yerself better hurry"

#### 3. Geordie (Newcastle/Tyneside)
Features of the Northeast:
- **Pronouns**: wor (our), yous, us (I/me)
- **Examples**: "Wor lass is canny", "Us are going to the match", "Where are yous going?"

#### 4. Scouse (Liverpool)
Liverpool dialect features:
- **Pronouns**: yous, youse, la (term of address)
- **Examples**: "Youse are sound, la", "That's boss, that is"

#### 5. Brummie (Birmingham)
Birmingham dialect features:
- **Pronouns**: yow (you), thee
- **Examples**: "Yow alright?", "Where's thee going?"

#### 6. Scottish English
Features of Scottish English:
- **Pronouns**: ye (you), youse (you plural)
- **Examples**: "Ye cannae do that", "Where are youse going?"
- **Modal**: cannae (cannot), dinnae (don't), wouldnae (wouldn't)

### 15. Collective Nouns

British English treats collective nouns as plural more often than US English:
- British: "The team are playing well"
- US: "The team is playing well"

Both forms are supported in the grammar.

### 16. Context-Sensitive Validation (11 rules)

1. **Subject-verb agreement**: Subject and verb must agree in number and person
2. **Pronoun case**: Nominative for subjects, accusative for objects
3. **Article-noun compatibility**: "a" vs "an" based on sound
4. **Reflexive pronoun agreement**: Must match subject in person and number
5. **Tense consistency**: Consistent tense usage within sentences
6. **British spelling consistency**: Warning for mixed British/American spelling
7. **Passive voice formation**: Proper use of be + past participle
8. **Modal verb + infinitive**: Modals followed by base form
9. **Comparative context**: Comparative adjectives with "than"
10. **Nested clause depth**: Warnings for deep nesting (>3 levels)

## Differences from US English

### Spelling
- British: colour, centre, organise, programme
- US: color, center, organize, program

### Past Tense
- British: learnt, spelt, burnt, dreamt
- US: learned, spelled, burned, dreamed

### Vocabulary
- British: lift, flat, lorry, petrol, autumn, biscuit
- US: elevator, apartment, truck, gas, fall, cookie

### Grammar
- British: "The team are playing" (collective noun as plural)
- US: "The team is playing" (collective noun as singular)

### Modals
- British: shan't, needn't (more common)
- US: won't, don't need to (more common)
